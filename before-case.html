Before the case

Dangerous Chest Pain

Start by thinking danger first. Your job is not to prove the benign cause. Your job is to avoid missing the life-threatening one.

Can kill fast Pattern 1 Next: Case 1

Today's aim

Recognise chest pain that needs urgent escalation.

Do not get trapped by age, anxiety, reflux symptoms, normal first ECG, or a reassuring appearance.

What this tests

Risk sorting under time pressure.

History, red flags, immediate action, safe language, and knowing when GP management is not enough.

Station type

Patient consultation.

Take focused history, explain likely diagnosis and differentials, then arrange urgent management.

Main danger

Missing acute coronary syndrome.

Treat concerning chest pain as cardiac until a safer pathway is clearly established.

Focus here

First action before full explanation.

If the story is high risk, say you are arranging urgent hospital assessment. Then explain why.

Four causes not to miss

1 Acute coronary syndrome
2 Aortic dissection
3 Pulmonary embolism
4 Tension pneumothorax

Fast trigger

Pressure chest pain + risk factors = urgent pathway.

Especially if exertional, radiating, associated with sweating, nausea, breathlessness, collapse, or ongoing pain.

First action

Escalate early.

Call ambulance or send to ED depending on setting, monitor if available, and avoid delaying for reassurance.

Do not do these

× Do not reassure based on a normal first ECG.
× Do not wait for troponin if the story is high risk.
× Do not give nitrate advice before checking sildenafil or similar medicines.
× Do not manage unstable chest pain as routine GP follow-up.

One sentence to remember

“This could be heart-related, so I am arranging urgent hospital assessment now.”

Warm-up

Say the first 20 seconds aloud.

Open safely, acknowledge the symptom, and move straight into focused risk questions.
