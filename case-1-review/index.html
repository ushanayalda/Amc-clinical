Case 1 · clinician review

Acute chest pain in a 60-year-old man

The complete station without reasoning prompts.

Stem

You are working in a hospital Emergency Department.

You are asked to see a 60-year-old man complaining of acute chest pain.

Tasks
  1. 1

    Take a concise, relevant and focused history.

  2. 2

    Present a summary of the patient's history to the examiner, who will then give you the findings on physical examination which you request.

  3. 3

    Tell the examiner your provisional diagnosis and the reasons for this.

  4. 4

    Interpret the ECG to the examiner. The ECG will be given to you at about 7 minutes into this consultation.

  5. 5

    Institute emergency treatment.

Full station

Spoken run

Opening and immediate safety

Action

Perform hand hygiene, enter calmly and observe the patient while approaching.

You

Hello, I am one of the doctors looking after you. Can I confirm your name and age?

Patient

Peter Lewis. I am 60.

You

Peter, I can see you are in pain. Is it okay if I ask focused questions while the team checks you?

Patient

Yes, please.

You

I would ask the team to repeat his vital signs, attach continuous cardiac monitoring, obtain intravenous access and perform an urgent 12-lead ECG while I continue the focused history.

You

Tell me what has happened.

Patient

I have a very bad tightness in my chest.

Focused history

You

Where exactly do you feel it?

Patient

It is in the middle of my chest, behind the breastbone.

You

Does it go anywhere else?

Patient

It goes up to my lower jaw.

You

What were you doing when it started?

Patient

I was playing tennis, in the third set.

You

When did it begin?

Patient

About two hours ago.

You

What has happened since it began?

Patient

It has stayed there and is steadily getting worse.

You

How severe is it from zero to ten?

Patient

About eight out of ten.

You

Is it worse when you breathe in?

Patient

No, it is not worse when I breathe.

You

Is it worse when you move or when I press on your chest?

Patient

No.

You

Has anything relieved it?

Patient

The Anginine and oxygen they gave me helped only a little.

You

Have you been sweating?

Patient

Yes, and I feel sick and short of breath.

You

Have you vomited?

Patient

No.

You

Any palpitations, dizziness or fainting?

Patient

No.

You

Have you had similar discomfort recently?

Patient

For two months I have had chest tightness when walking on cold mornings.

You

Any recent breathlessness when walking or lying down?

Patient

I get short of breath with exertion, and I have been short of breath at night for three days.

You

Have you had a heart attack or other heart problem before?

Patient

I had a heart attack three years ago.

You

Do you have diabetes, high blood pressure or high cholesterol?

Patient

I have diabetes, high blood pressure and high cholesterol.

You

Do you smoke or drink alcohol?

Patient

I used to smoke but stopped three years ago. I have one glass of wine a day.

You

Do you get pain in either leg when walking?

Patient

My left leg hurts after about 500 metres.

You

What medicines do you take?

Patient

Tablets for blood pressure, metformin, aspirin and a cholesterol tablet.

You

Do you have any medicine allergies?

Patient

None that I know of.

You

Any indigestion, reflux, pain after meals or trouble swallowing?

Patient

No.

You

Have you noticed black or bloody bowel motions?

Patient

My bowel motions have been black for the last five days.

You

Thank you. I will act on both the chest pain and the possible bleeding.

Summary, examination and provisional diagnosis

You

In summary, Peter is a 60-year-old man with two hours of severe, steadily worsening central chest tightness radiating to the jaw, beginning during tennis and associated with sweating, nausea and breathlessness. He has two months of exertional chest discomfort, previous myocardial infarction, diabetes, hypertension, hypercholesterolaemia, previous smoking and probable peripheral vascular disease. He also reports five days of black bowel motions, raising concern for gastrointestinal bleeding and possible anaemia.

You

Please provide his current vital signs, general appearance, peripheral perfusion, cardiovascular and respiratory examination findings, including any signs of heart failure.

Examiner

He is overweight, anxious, sweating and in acute distress. His blood pressure is 150/96 mmHg and his pulse is regular at 96 per minute. Heart sounds are dual with no murmur. There are no clinical signs of heart failure. The remainder of the examination is noncontributory.

You

My provisional diagnosis is acute coronary syndrome, most likely an acute myocardial infarction. This is supported by the prolonged worsening exertional central tightness, jaw radiation, autonomic symptoms, previous infarction and multiple vascular risk factors. His probable melaena is an important concurrent bleeding concern.

ECG and emergency treatment

Examiner

At about seven minutes, the 12-lead ECG is provided. Please interpret it.

You

This ECG shows sinus rhythm at about 96 per minute. There are Q waves and ST-segment elevation in leads II, III and aVF. In this clinical context, this is an acute inferior STEMI.

You

I would call the senior emergency doctor and cardiology team, activate the local STEMI pathway, continue cardiac monitoring, keep defibrillation available, maintain intravenous access and repeat ABCDE and vital signs.

You

I would check his oxygen saturation and give oxygen only for hypoxaemia, shock, heart failure or another current indication.

You

For pain, I would consider nitrate only after checking blood pressure, right-ventricular involvement and contraindications. I would reserve opioid for significant pain persisting despite appropriate nitrate use.

You

Aspirin is normally given early after contraindication assessment. Here, probable melaena makes antiplatelet and reperfusion decisions non-routine. I would clarify active bleeding, obtain immediate senior and cardiac-expert advice, and avoid unqualified fibrinolysis.

You

I would send full blood count, group and hold, coagulation studies, renal function, glucose and high-sensitivity troponin without waiting for biomarkers before pathway activation.

You

I would confirm the local PCI and transfer pathway now. Primary PCI is preferred when achievable within 120 minutes of first medical contact. Recent probable gastrointestinal bleeding makes fibrinolysis unsafe without expert resolution.

You

Peter, the ECG shows a serious heart attack requiring urgent specialist treatment. We are also assessing the possible gastrointestinal bleeding.

You

My handover: Peter is 60 with a two-hour acute inferior STEMI and is not currently in shock or heart failure. The STEMI pathway is active. Five days of probable melaena create unresolved antiplatelet and fibrinolysis risk. We need urgent PCI-pathway confirmation, monitored care and parallel blood testing without delaying reperfusion planning.
