Case 1 - Classic Chest Pain in General Practice

Details
Pattern:
Dangerous Chest Pain
Clinical Variant:
Classic acute coronary syndrome
Station Type:
Patient Consultation
Task Recipient:
Patient
Setting:
General Practice
Main Risk Point:
Do not delay ambulance transfer while trying to prove the diagnosis.

Section 1 - Station Stem

You are a General Practitioner in a suburban clinic.

Mr Peter Lewis is a 60-year-old man who presents with central chest pressure.

The pain started 40 minutes ago while he was walking from the car park to your clinic.

He looks pale and sweaty.

Past history:
Hypertension.

Medication:
Perindopril.

No investigation results are available at the start of the station.

Your tasks are to:

  1. Take a focused history.
  2. Explain your most likely dangerous working diagnosis.
  3. Explain important differential diagnoses.
  4. Outline your immediate management plan.

You have 8 minutes.

Section 2 - 2-Minute Reading Map

Setting:
General Practice.

Patient:
60-year-old man.

Problem:
Central chest pressure for 40 minutes.

Already concerning:
Pale.
Sweaty.
Started with walking.

Four causes not to miss:
Acute coronary syndrome.
Aortic dissection.
Pulmonary embolism.
Tension pneumothorax.

Most likely dangerous working diagnosis:
Heart-related chest pain.

First action:
Arrange ambulance transfer now.
Do a heart tracing if available and not delaying transfer.

Main risk point:
Do not delay transfer while trying to prove the diagnosis.

Must ask:
Where is the pain?
Does it spread?
Sweating, nausea, breathlessness, fainting?
Severe pain to the back?
Coughing blood or clot risk?
Sudden sharp pain with severe breathlessness?
Heart risk factors?
Aspirin allergy or bleeding risk?

Must say:
“This is not safe to manage in this clinic. I am arranging an ambulance now.”

Section 3 - Live AMC Station Script

Phase 1 - Introduction and Consent

Candidate:

“Hello, I am Dr ___. How would you like me to address you?”

Patient: “Call me Mr Lewis.”

Candidate:

“Thank you, Mr Lewis. I can see you look pale and sweaty.”

“I am worried this may be coming from your heart.”

“I am going to arrange an ambulance while I ask you a few focused questions. Is that okay?”

Patient: “An ambulance? Is it that serious?”

Candidate:

“It may be serious. It does not mean you definitely have a heart attack, but it is serious enough that we should not wait.”

Patient: “Okay.”

Phase 2 - Open Question

Candidate:

“Tell me what happened today.”

Patient: “I was walking from the car park about 40 minutes ago and felt pressure in the middle of my chest.”

Candidate:

“Thank you. I will keep the questions focused.”

Phase 3 - Focused History

Candidate:

“Is the pressure still there now?”

Patient: “Yes.”

Candidate:

“Where exactly is it?”

Patient: “In the centre of my chest.”

Candidate:

“Does it spread anywhere?”

Patient: “To my left arm.”

Candidate:

“Does it go to your jaw, neck, back, shoulder, or upper tummy?”

Patient: “A bit to my jaw. Not to my back.”

Candidate:

“How bad is it out of ten?”

Patient: “About eight.”

Candidate:

“Did it start suddenly or build up?”

Patient: “It built up over a few minutes while I was walking.”

Candidate:

“Have you felt sweaty, sick, vomited, short of breath, faint, or had a racing heartbeat?”

Patient: “I am sweaty and feel sick. I am a bit short of breath. I have not fainted.”

Candidate:

“Any severe tearing pain going through to your back?”

Patient: “No.”

Candidate:

“Any coughing blood?”

Patient: “No.”

Candidate:

“Any calf pain, leg swelling, recent long travel, recent surgery, being stuck in bed, cancer, or previous blood clot?”

Patient: “No.”

Candidate:

“Any sudden sharp chest pain with severe breathlessness?”

Patient: “No. It feels more like pressure.”

Candidate:

“Do you have diabetes, high cholesterol, kidney disease, previous heart disease, or smoking history?”

Patient: “I have high blood pressure. I used to smoke but stopped five years ago.”

Candidate:

“Any close family history of heart disease?”

Patient: “My father had a heart attack in his fifties.”

Candidate:

“What medicines do you take?”

Patient: “Perindopril for blood pressure.”

Candidate:

“Are you allergic to aspirin?”

Patient: “No.”

Candidate:

“Any active bleeding, stomach bleeding, bleeding disorder, or blood thinner medicine?”

Patient: “No.”

Candidate:

“Thank you. I have enough information to act safely now.”

Phase 4 - Red Flag Sweep

Candidate:

“Just to check the danger signs clearly: the pain is still there, it spreads to your left arm and jaw, and you feel sweaty, sick, and a bit short of breath. Correct?”

Patient: “Yes.”

Candidate:

“And no severe tearing pain to the back, no collapse, no coughing blood, and no one-sided leg swelling?”

Patient: “Correct.”

Candidate:

“That pattern is concerning. I am treating this as urgent.”

Phase 5 - Structured Summary

Candidate:

“Let me summarise.”

“You developed central chest pressure about 40 minutes ago while walking. It is still there. It spreads to your left arm and jaw, and you feel sweaty, sick, and a bit short of breath.”

“You have high blood pressure, past smoking, and a family history of heart disease.”

“Is that right?”

Patient: “Yes.”

Phase 6 - Diagnosis Explanation

Candidate:

“The main concern is that this may be coming from your heart.”

“That includes a possible heart attack or unstable blood flow to the heart.”

“I cannot confirm that here in the clinic.”

“But your symptoms are serious enough that I need to treat this as a possible heart problem now.”

Patient: “So you think it could be my heart?”

Candidate:

“Yes. That is why I am arranging urgent hospital care.”

Phase 7 - Differential Explanation

Candidate:

“There are a few other serious causes of chest pain that hospital also needs to check.”

“One is a tear in the main blood vessel from the heart.”

“One is a blood clot in the lung.”

“One is a collapsed lung.”

“Indigestion or muscle pain can happen too, but with your symptoms I cannot safely call it that here.”

Patient: “I thought it might just be indigestion.”

Candidate:

“It may turn out to be something less serious, but we need to check the dangerous causes first.”

Phase 8 - Decision Point and Management Plan

Phase 8A - Decision Point

Candidate:

“Because the pressure is still there, it spreads to your arm and jaw, and you feel sweaty, sick, and short of breath, I am treating this as a possible heart problem now.”

“I am not waiting for certainty before acting.”

Phase 8B - Management Plan

Candidate:

“This is not safe to manage in this clinic.”

“I am arranging an ambulance now.”

“You should not drive yourself.”

Patient: “Can my wife drive me? It is only nearby.”

Candidate:

“No. You need an ambulance because they can monitor you and act quickly if your condition changes.”

“While we wait, we will keep you resting and check your blood pressure, pulse, oxygen level, breathing rate, temperature, and alertness.”

“If we can do a heart tracing immediately without delaying the ambulance, we will do it.”

“I will give aspirin if it is safe for you. You have no aspirin allergy and no bleeding concern, so I would give it now.”

“I will give oxygen only if your oxygen level is low or you show signs that you need it.”

“Before any chest pain spray or tablet under the tongue, I will check your blood pressure and make sure none of your recent medicines make it unsafe.”

“I will not delay the ambulance for blood tests that need to happen in hospital.”

“In hospital, they can monitor your heart, repeat heart tracings, do urgent blood tests, and give treatment if needed.”

Phase 9 - Urgency Classification

Candidate:

“This is urgent.”

“The safest plan is ambulance transfer now.”

Patient: “I am worried.”

Candidate:

“I understand. We are acting early to keep you safe.”

Phase 10 - Safety Net

Candidate:

“While we wait, tell me immediately if the pain gets worse, you feel faint, become more breathless, feel very clammy, or feel like you might collapse.”

“Please stay seated or lying down.”

“Do not walk around.”

Patient: “Okay.”

Phase 11 - Check Understanding

Candidate:

“Can you tell me what you understand about why I am arranging an ambulance?”

Patient: “You are worried this could be my heart, and I need hospital urgently.”

Candidate:

“That is right. I will stay with you and hand over clearly to the ambulance team.”

Section 4 - Doctor Thinking - Hidden

Display:

Dangerous working diagnosis:
Possible acute coronary syndrome.

Patient wording:
Possible heart problem.
Possible heart attack.
Unstable blood flow to the heart.

Why it is likely:
Central chest pressure.
Exertional onset.
Ongoing pain.
Radiation to left arm and jaw.
Sweating.
Nausea.
Breathlessness.
Age 60.
Hypertension.
Past smoking.
Family history.

Four causes not to miss:
Acute coronary syndrome.
Aortic dissection.
Pulmonary embolism.
Tension pneumothorax.

Aortic dissection less likely because:
No sudden maximal tearing pain.
No pain through to the back.
No neurological symptoms.
No collapse.

Pulmonary embolism less likely because:
No pleuritic pain.
No haemoptysis.
No calf swelling.
No travel, surgery, immobility, cancer, or previous clot.

Tension pneumothorax less likely because:
Pain is pressure-like, not sudden sharp pleuritic pain.
No severe unilateral breathlessness.
No trauma.
No one-sided chest signs provided.

General Practice logic:
This cannot be safely ruled out in clinic.
Call ambulance.
Do a heart tracing only if immediate and not delaying transfer.
Do not wait for troponin.
Do not allow private transport.

Medication safety:
Aspirin if no allergy or bleeding concern.
Oxygen only if low oxygen, heart failure, shock, severe respiratory distress, or poor perfusion.
Chest pain spray or tablet only after blood pressure and medication safety check.
Recent sildenafil or tadalafil must be checked before glyceryl trinitrate.

Decision Point:
The moment central chest pressure is associated with radiation, sweating, nausea, breathlessness, ongoing symptoms, and cardiac risk factors, treat as possible acute coronary syndrome until proven otherwise.

Must-Say Lines

“I am arranging an ambulance now.”

“You should not drive yourself.”

“I am treating this as a possible heart problem now.”

“I will not delay transfer for troponin.”

“I will give aspirin if safe.”

“I will give oxygen only if clinically indicated.”

“I will check blood pressure and medication safety before any chest pain spray or tablet.”

Section 6 - Practice Drill

30-Second Compression

“Mr Lewis has possible heart-related chest pain because he has central chest pressure for 40 minutes, spreading to the left arm and jaw, with sweating, nausea, breathlessness, exertional onset, and heart risk factors. In General Practice, I will arrange an ambulance now, give aspirin if safe, do a heart tracing only if it does not delay transfer, give oxygen only if indicated, check safety before any chest pain spray or tablet, and not delay transfer for troponin.”

10-Second Trigger Rule

“Central chest pressure with sweating, radiation, nausea, breathlessness, or risk factors is a possible heart problem until proven otherwise.”

Differential Ranking

1. Most likely diagnosis:
Acute coronary syndrome.

2. Dangerous alternative:
Aortic dissection or pulmonary embolism.

3. Main mimic:
Reflux or muscle pain.

Why Not the Main Mimic?

“Reflux or muscle pain may be possible, but they do not safely explain exertional central chest pressure with arm and jaw radiation, sweating, nausea, breathlessness, and heart risk factors.”

First Action Rule

“In General Practice, arrange an ambulance now and do a heart tracing only if it does not delay transfer.”
