Case 1

Classic Chest Pain in General Practice

Classic acute coronary syndrome.

Phase 1 - Can Kill Fast Pattern 1 Patient Consultation

Case Header

Pattern

Dangerous Chest Pain

Clinical Variant

Classic acute coronary syndrome

Station Type

Patient Consultation

Task Recipient

Patient

Setting

General Practice

Main Risk Point

Do not delay ambulance transfer while trying to prove the diagnosis.

Section 1 - Station Stem

You are a General Practitioner in a suburban clinic.

Mr Peter Lewis is 60 years old.

He presents with central chest pressure that started 40 minutes ago while walking from the car park.

He looks pale and sweaty.

Past history

Hypertension.

Medication

Perindopril.

Your tasks are to:

  1. Take a focused history.
  2. Explain the most likely dangerous working diagnosis.
  3. Explain important differential diagnoses.
  4. Explain your immediate management plan.

You have 8 minutes.

Section 2 - 2-Minute Reading Map

Task Recipient

Patient.

Use calm everyday language.

Pattern Trigger

Central chest pressure for 40 minutes.

Pale, sweaty, exertional onset, age 60, hypertension.

Four Causes Not to Miss

  • Acute coronary syndrome

    Patient wording:
    heart-related chest pain
  • Aortic dissection
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Tension pneumothorax

Decision Point

Chest pressure plus sweating, radiation, nausea, breathlessness, collapse, or risk factors.

Treat as a possible heart problem now.

General Practice First Action

Call ambulance now.

Do an electrocardiogram immediately if available and not delaying transfer.

Do Not Do These

  • Do not delay transfer for troponin.
  • Do not let the patient drive.
  • Do not call it reflux.
  • Do not give oxygen routinely.

How to Speak

  • Say:
    “possible heart problem”
  • Say:
    “heart tracing”
  • Say:
    “urgent hospital assessment”
  • Avoid technical pathway language.

Section 3 - Spoken Script

Phase 1 - Introduction and Consent

“Hello Mr Lewis, I’m Dr ___. I can see you look pale and sweaty.”

“Chest pressure can sometimes come from the heart, so I need to ask focused questions and check your observations urgently.”

“Is it okay if I do that while my nurse arranges an ambulance?”

Patient

“Yes.”

Phase 2 - Open Question

“Tell me what happened from the start.”

Patient

“I was walking from the car park about 40 minutes ago and felt pressure in the middle of my chest.”

Phase 3 - Focused History

Must Ask

  • “Is the pressure still there now?”
  • “Does it go to your arm, jaw, neck, back, shoulder, or upper tummy?”
  • “How severe is it out of ten?”
  • “Did it start suddenly or build up?”
  • “Were you walking, resting, eating, or doing anything strenuous?”
  • “Have you felt sweaty, nauseated, vomited, breathless, faint, or had palpitations?”
  • “Any severe tearing pain going through to the back?”
  • “Any coughing blood, calf pain, leg swelling, recent surgery, long travel, or previous blood clot?”
  • “Any sudden sharp chest pain with severe breathlessness?”
  • “Do you have diabetes, high cholesterol, kidney disease, previous heart disease, or smoking history?”
  • “Are you allergic to aspirin, or do you have active bleeding or take blood thinners?”
  • “Have you taken medicines for erectile dysfunction, such as sildenafil or tadalafil, recently?”

If Time

  • “Have you had this pain before?”
  • “Any family history of heart disease at a young age?”
  • “What medicines do you take?”
  • “Any recent chest injury?”
  • “Any fever or cough?”

Phase 4 - Red Flag Sweep

“Mr Lewis, I need to quickly confirm the danger signs.”

“Is the pain getting worse?”

“Are you feeling faint, very breathless, clammy, or like you might collapse?”

“Any severe pain to the back?”

“Any new weakness, face droop, or speech trouble?”

Patient

“The pain is still there. It goes to my left arm. I feel sweaty and sick.”

Doctor

“That is concerning. I am treating this as urgent.”

Phase 5 - Structured Summary

“Mr Lewis, you have central chest pressure that started while walking and has lasted about 40 minutes.”

“It is still there, it goes to your left arm, and you feel sweaty and nauseated.”

“You also have high blood pressure.”

“This pattern is concerning for pain coming from the heart.”

Phase 6 - Diagnosis Explanation

“The most important possibility is heart-related chest pain.”

“That includes a possible heart attack or unstable blood flow to the heart muscle.”

“This does not mean you definitely have a heart attack.”

“But it is serious enough that I must treat it as a possible heart problem until hospital tests prove otherwise.”

Phase 7 - Differential Explanation

“There are other serious causes I also need to keep in mind.”

“A tear in the main blood vessel from the heart can cause sudden severe pain going to the back.”

“A blood clot in the lung can cause chest pain with breathlessness or low oxygen.”

“A collapsed lung can cause sudden sharp chest pain and severe breathlessness.”

“Reflux, muscle pain, or anxiety can also cause chest symptoms, but I cannot safely call it that here.”

Phase 8 - Decision Point and Management Plan

Phase 8A - Decision Point

“Because of the central chest pressure, sweating, nausea, radiation to your arm, and your risk factors, I am treating this as a possible heart problem now.”

“I am not waiting for certainty before acting.”

Phase 8B - Management Plan

“This is not safe to manage in this clinic.”

“I am arranging an ambulance now.”

“You should not drive yourself.”

“While we wait, we will keep you resting and check your blood pressure, pulse, oxygen level, breathing rate, temperature, and conscious state.”

“If we can do a heart tracing immediately without delaying the ambulance, we will do it.”

“I will give aspirin if it is safe for you.”

“I will give oxygen only if your oxygen level is low or you show signs that you need it.”

“If I use a spray or tablet under the tongue for chest pain, I will first check your blood pressure and make sure there is no medication risk.”

“In hospital, they can monitor your heart, repeat heart tracings, do blood tests, and give urgent treatment if needed.”

Phase 9 - Urgency Classification

“This is urgent.”

“The safest plan is ambulance transfer now.”

Phase 10 - Safety Net

“While we wait, please tell me immediately if the pain worsens, you feel faint, become more breathless, feel clammy, or feel like you might collapse.”

“Please stay seated or lying down.”

Phase 11 - Check Understanding

“Can you tell me what you understand about why I am arranging an ambulance?”

Patient

“You are worried this could be my heart, and I need hospital assessment urgently.”

Doctor

“That is right. I will stay with you and hand over clearly to the ambulance team.”

Section 4 - Doctor Thinking - Hidden

Stability

Pale and sweaty with ongoing central chest pressure.

Check:

  • Airway
  • Breathing
  • Circulation
  • Conscious state
  • Blood pressure
  • Pulse
  • Oxygen saturation
  • Perfusion

Stop the history if he deteriorates.

Dangerous Working Diagnosis

Possible acute coronary syndrome.

Patient-facing wording:

Possible heart problem.

Possible heart attack or unstable heart pain.

Four Causes Not to Miss

  • Acute coronary syndrome:

    Likely because of central pressure, exertional onset, sweating, nausea, radiation, ongoing pain, age, and hypertension.
  • Aortic dissection:

    Ask for sudden maximal tearing pain, back pain, collapse, neurological symptoms, or pulse difference.
  • Pulmonary embolism:

    Ask for pleuritic pain, sudden breathlessness, haemoptysis, calf swelling, recent surgery, immobility, cancer, or previous clot.
  • Tension pneumothorax:

    Ask for sudden sharp chest pain, severe breathlessness, one-sided chest signs, trauma, or deterioration.

Medication Safety

  • Aspirin:

    Give if suspected heart-related chest pain and no true allergy or active bleeding concern.
  • Glyceryl trinitrate:

    Check blood pressure, shock, and recent sildenafil or tadalafil.
  • Oxygen:

    Use only if hypoxic, shocked, in heart failure, or poorly perfused.

Main Risk Points

  • Do not call it reflux.
  • Do not let him drive.
  • Do not wait for troponin in General Practice.
  • Do not give routine oxygen.
  • Do not give glyceryl trinitrate without checking blood pressure and medication safety.
  • Do not use a normal first electrocardiogram to dismiss the history.

Decision Point

The exact Decision Point is:

The moment central chest pressure has radiation, sweating, nausea, breathlessness, ongoing symptoms, abnormal observations, or cardiac risk factors, I treat it as possible acute coronary syndrome until proven otherwise.

Must-Say Lines

  • “This is not safe to manage in this clinic.”
  • “I am arranging an ambulance now.”
  • “I will not delay transfer for troponin.”
  • “I will give aspirin if safe.”
  • “I will check blood pressure and medication safety before glyceryl trinitrate.”
  • “I will give oxygen only if clinically indicated.”
Section 5 - Examiner Lines - Hidden
  • “My working diagnosis is possible acute coronary syndrome until proven otherwise.”
  • “In patient language, I will explain this as possible heart-related chest pain.”
  • “This is not safe to manage in General Practice.”
  • “I will arrange urgent ambulance transfer now.”
  • “I will not delay transfer for troponin.”
  • “I will do an electrocardiogram only if immediately available and not delaying transfer.”
  • “I will give aspirin if suspected acute coronary syndrome and no contraindication.”
  • “I will check blood pressure and contraindications before glyceryl trinitrate.”
  • “I will give oxygen only if the patient is hypoxic, in heart failure, in shock, or poorly perfused.”
  • “I will actively consider aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, and tension pneumothorax.”
  • “You should not drive yourself.”

Section 6 - Practice Drill

1. Thirty Second Compression

“This is possible acute coronary syndrome because he has central chest pressure with exertional onset, sweating, nausea, radiation, ongoing symptoms, and risk factors. In General Practice I will call an ambulance now, give aspirin if safe, check safety before glyceryl trinitrate, give oxygen only if indicated, and not delay transfer for troponin.”

2. Ten Second Trigger Rule

“Chest pressure with sweating, radiation, nausea, breathlessness, collapse, or risk factors is a possible heart problem until proven otherwise.”

3. Differential Ranking

  1. Most likely diagnosis:

    Possible acute coronary syndrome.
  2. Dangerous alternative:

    Aortic dissection or pulmonary embolism.
  3. Main mimic:

    Reflux or musculoskeletal chest pain.

4. Why Not the Main Mimic

“Reflux or muscle pain may be possible, but they do not safely explain exertional onset, radiation to the arm, sweating, nausea, ongoing pain, pallor, or cardiac risk factors.”

5. First Action Rule

“In General Practice, first assess stability and call an ambulance. Do not delay transfer for troponin.”

Section 7 - Full Spoken Run

“Hello Mr Lewis, I’m Dr ___. I can see you look pale and sweaty. Chest pressure can sometimes come from the heart, so I need to ask focused questions and check your observations urgently.”

“Is it okay if I do that while my nurse arranges an ambulance?”

“Tell me what happened from the start.”

“So it started about 40 minutes ago while you were walking, and it feels like pressure in the middle of your chest.”

“Is it still there now? Does it go anywhere, such as your arm, jaw, neck, back, shoulder, or upper tummy?”

“Have you felt sweaty, nauseated, breathless, faint, or had any racing heartbeat?”

“Was there severe tearing pain going to the back?”

“Any coughing blood, calf pain, leg swelling, recent surgery, long travel, or previous blood clot?”

“Do you have diabetes, high cholesterol, kidney disease, previous heart disease, smoking history, or family history of early heart disease?”

“Are you allergic to aspirin, or do you have active bleeding or take blood thinners?”

“Have you taken medicines for erectile dysfunction, such as sildenafil or tadalafil, recently?”

“Let me summarise. You have central chest pressure that is still present. It started while walking, goes to your left arm, and you feel sweaty and nauseated. You also have high blood pressure.”

“The most important possibility is heart-related chest pain, including a possible heart attack or unstable blood flow to the heart muscle. This does not mean you definitely have a heart attack, but I must treat it as a possible heart problem until hospital tests prove otherwise.”

“I also need to keep in mind other serious causes, such as a tear in the main blood vessel from the heart, a blood clot in the lung, or a collapsed lung. Reflux or muscle pain is possible, but I cannot safely call it that here.”

“Because of your symptoms and risk factors, I am treating this as a possible heart problem now. I am not waiting for certainty before acting.”

“This is not safe to manage in this clinic. I am arranging an ambulance now. You should not drive yourself.”

“While we wait, we will keep you resting, check your observations, and do a heart tracing if it is immediately available without delaying the ambulance.”

“I will give aspirin if it is safe for you. I will give oxygen only if your oxygen level is low or you show signs that you need it.”

“If I use a spray or tablet under the tongue for chest pain, I will first check your blood pressure and make sure there is no medication risk.”

“In hospital, they can monitor your heart, repeat heart tracings, do blood tests, and give urgent treatment if needed.”

“Please tell me immediately if the pain worsens, you feel faint, become more breathless, feel clammy, or feel like you might collapse.”

“Can you tell me what you understand about why I am arranging an ambulance?”

“That is right. I am worried this could be coming from your heart and needs hospital assessment urgently. I will stay with you and hand over clearly to the ambulance team.”
