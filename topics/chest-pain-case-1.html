SECTION 1 – STATION STEM

You are a GP in a suburban clinic.

A 60-year-old man presents with central chest pressure for 40 minutes.

Past medical history: Hypertension.

Medications: Amlodipine.

During history, he mentions taking sildenafil last night.

Tasks:

  1. Take a focused history.
  2. Explain your most likely diagnosis and differential diagnoses.
  3. Outline your management plan including medication safety.

You have 8 minutes.

SECTION 2 – 2 MINUTE READING MAP

Setting: GP

Central pressure
Radiation likely
Autonomic symptoms

Immediate threats:
Acute coronary syndrome
Aortic dissection
Pulmonary embolism

First action rule: ECG immediately

Critical trap: PDE5 use and nitrates

Urgency: Emergency transfer

PHASE 1 – Introduction and Consent

Hello, I am Dr Ushana.

PHASE 2 – Open Question

Can you tell me what happened today?
Patient: “I developed pressure in my chest about 40 minutes ago.”

PHASE 3 – Focused History

Was it sudden?
Patient: “Yes.”
Where exactly is the pain?
Patient: “In the centre.”
Does it spread anywhere?
Patient: “To my left arm.”
Any sweating or nausea?
Patient: “Yes.”
Any shortness of breath?
Patient: “Yes.”
Any tearing pain to your back?
Patient: “No.”
Any recent long travel?
Patient: “No.”

Patient: “No.”
Have you taken any medication for erectile dysfunction recently?
Patient: “Yes, last night.”
Thank you for telling me. That is important.

PHASE 4 – Red Flag Sweep

Just to confirm, no collapse and no severe tearing back pain. Correct?
Patient: “Correct.”

PHASE 5 – Structured Summary

Let me summarise to ensure I understood correctly.
You developed sudden central chest pressure 40 minutes ago, radiating to your arm, associated with sweating and shortness of breath. You have hypertension and took sildenafil last night. Is that accurate?
Patient: “Yes.”

PHASE 6 – Diagnosis Explanation

Based on what you have described, the most likely diagnosis is acute coronary syndrome.
SCORING PHRASE Sudden central chest pressure with autonomic symptoms must be treated as acute coronary syndrome until proven otherwise.

PHASE 7 – Differential Explanation

Other possibilities include pulmonary embolism and aortic dissection, but your symptom pattern strongly suggests a cardiac cause.

PHASE 8 – Management Plan

We are in a GP setting.
FOCUS HERE
This is an emergency.
The first step is to perform an ECG immediately.
SCORING PHRASE ECG must be done immediately in suspected acute coronary syndrome.
I will give aspirin now since you have no allergy.
However, because you took sildenafil recently, I will not give nitrates.
SCORING PHRASE Nitrates are contraindicated in patients who have recently taken sildenafil due to risk of severe hypotension.
You require immediate ambulance transfer to hospital.
We will not delay transfer for investigations that must be done in hospital.
Oxygen will only be given if your oxygen level is low or you develop respiratory distress.

PHASE 9 – Urgency Classification

This is an emergency requiring immediate hospital transfer.

PHASE 10 – Safety Net

If your pain worsens or you feel faint while waiting, emergency services are being arranged and we will act immediately.

PHASE 11 – Check Understanding

Do you understand why we must avoid certain medications and transfer urgently?
Patient: “Yes.”

SECTION 6 – MASTERY DRILL

30-Second Compression
Central chest pressure with radiation and autonomic symptoms in a high-risk patient. Most likely acute coronary syndrome. Recent sildenafil means no nitrates. ECG now and immediate ambulance transfer.
10-Second Trigger Rule
Chest pain plus PDE5 use equals no nitrates.
Differential Ranking
1) Acute coronary syndrome
2) Pulmonary embolism
3) Aortic dissection
Why Not Pulmonary Embolism?
Symptom pattern is central pressure with radiation and autonomic symptoms rather than pleuritic pain.
First Action Rule
ECG immediately and arrange ambulance transfer. Avoid nitrates due to sildenafil.
