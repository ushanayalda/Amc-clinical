AMC Clinical - Chest pain - Tier 1 Dominance

Case 1 - Classic ACS - Strict AMC format

This file contains three setting variants. Content blocks change by setting while structure stays fixed.

SECTION 1 - STATION STEM

You are a GP in a suburban clinic.

A 58-year-old man presents with central chest pain for 1 hour.

Past medical history:

  • Hypertension
  • Dyslipidaemia

Medications:

  • Perindopril
  • Atorvastatin

He is a current smoker.

Tasks:

  1. Take a focused history.
  2. Explain your most likely diagnosis and differential diagnoses.
  3. Outline your management plan.

You have 8 minutes.

You are a doctor in an Emergency Department cubicle.

A 58-year-old man presents with central chest pain for 1 hour. He appears sweaty and uncomfortable.

Past medical history:

  • Dyslipidaemia

Medications:

  • Perindopril
  • Atorvastatin

He is a current smoker.

Tasks:

  2. Explain your most likely diagnosis and differential diagnoses.
  3. Outline your immediate management plan in ED.

You have 8 minutes.

Past medical history:

  • Dyslipidaemia

SECTION 2 - 2 MINUTE READING MAP

Setting: GP

Immediate threats to exclude:

  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Aortic dissection
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Tension pneumothorax

First action rule: ECG immediately

Key red flag questions: radiation, sweating, syncope, tearing back pain, recent travel, calf swelling, pleuritic pain

Likely direction: ACS

Urgency: emergency transfer

FOCUS HERE
In GP, do not delay transfer for hospital-only tests such as troponin.

Setting: ED

Immediate threats to exclude:

  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Aortic dissection
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Tension pneumothorax

First action rule: ECG within 10 minutes, monitor, IV access

Key red flag questions: radiation, sweating, syncope, tearing back pain, pleuritic pain, calf swelling, recent travel, neurological symptoms

Likely direction: ACS

Urgency: time critical ED pathway

FOCUS HERE
Stabilise first. Investigate second. Treat based on ECG pathway (STEMI vs NSTEMI).

Setting: Ward

Immediate threats to exclude:

  • Acute coronary syndrome
  • Aortic dissection
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Tension pneumothorax

First action rule: assess stability, call for help early, ECG now

Key red flag questions: ongoing pain at rest, dyspnoea, syncope, tearing back pain, pleuritic pain, calf swelling, recent surgery, haemodynamic instability

Likely direction: ACS until excluded

Urgency: escalate to senior and rapid response if unstable

FOCUS HERE
Ward chest pain is deterioration until proven otherwise. Escalate early.

PHASE 6 - Diagnosis Explanation

Based on what you have told me, the most likely diagnosis is acute coronary syndrome, meaning reduced blood flow to the heart muscle.
SCORING PHRASE Sudden central chest pressure with radiation and autonomic symptoms must be treated as acute coronary syndrome until proven otherwise.

PHASE 7 - Differential Explanation

Other serious causes we must consider include:
  • Pulmonary embolism, which is a blood clot in the lungs.
  • Aortic dissection, which is a tear in the main artery.
However, your symptom pattern strongly suggests a cardiac cause.
